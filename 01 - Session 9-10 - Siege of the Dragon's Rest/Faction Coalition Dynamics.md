# Faction Coalition Dynamics — The Siege

## The Unstable Coalition

Six factions surround the Dragon's Rest. They are not allies. They are rivals forced into cooperation by desperation — the party holds both the Key and the shard, and no single faction can breach the Dragon's Rest alone. The coalition is held together by two things: fear that the party will escape into Duur'Sharaat, and the unspoken agreement that they'll sort out who gets the prize *after* the pub falls.

Every faction has a reason to betray every other faction. The party's strategic goal is to find those reasons and trigger the betrayals.

## Fault Lines — The Tensions to Exploit

Each fault line below can be triggered by the party through roleplay, skill checks, or clever tactics. When a fault line breaks, it creates chaos in the coalition — reducing enemy numbers, redirecting attacks, or removing an entire faction from the siege.

### 1. Boromar vs. Daask — "The Cogs War"
**The tension:** Boromar and Daask have been fighting a shadow war for control of Sharn's underworld for years. They hate each other. The only reason they're standing on opposite sides of the same bridge without killing each other is that Corrin and Garra are both professionals. But professionalism has limits.

**How the party exploits it:**
- Plant evidence that Daask is planning to betray Boromar after the siege (forgery DC 14, or just spread the rumor with a DC 15 Charisma (Deception) check delivered via Tyrant or urchin).
- Kill a Boromar enforcer and make it look like Daask did it — claw marks, a harpy feather left at the scene.
- Have a party member with criminal contacts negotiate directly with Corrin: "Garra's planning to feed your people to the troll. We heard her say it." (DC 15 Charisma (Persuasion) if true, DC 18 if false).
- During a lull in fighting, cast *minor illusion* to project Garra's voice ordering Daask forces to "kill the halflings first."

**What happens when it breaks:**
- Boromar and Daask forces engage each other in the street. 2d4 combatants from each side are removed from the siege per round as they kill each other.
- The clawfoot dinosaurs and the troll fight each other — a kaiju battle on Bridge 47. Both are removed from the siege entirely.
- The coalition fractures. Corrin shouts: "This was a mistake!" and Boromar begins withdrawing — unless the party attacked them directly. Then it's personal.

### 2. Tarkanan vs. The Dragonmarked — "Blood Debts"
**The tension:** House Tarkanan exists to destroy the dragonmarked houses. Every Deneith marshal on site carries a dragonmark. Every Aurum mercenary is likely marked or employed by a marked house. Oleander is standing on a rooftop watching his sworn enemies and doing nothing. He will not do nothing forever.

**How the party exploits it:**
- Point Oleander at the Deneith marshals: "They're here to confiscate the schema for the Twelve. You know what that means." (DC 13 Charisma (Persuasion) — this is basically true).
- Point Oleander at Lord Vex's skycoach: "The Aurum is funding the next generation of dragonmarked weapons research. Vex has the contracts in his coat pocket." (DC 16 Deception, or DC 13 if the party has actual intelligence about Aurum dealings).
- Give Oleander a target he can't refuse: if the party has an Aberrant Mark Token from him, using it in view of the Deneith marshals is a declaration. Tarkanan responds.

**What happens when it breaks:**
- Tarkanan Shroud operatives assassinate 1d3 Deneith marshals in a single coordinated strike. The remaining marshals retreat or call for reinforcements.
- Oleander descends from the rooftop and engages the Aurum skycoach. Lord Vex's war mage and Oleander duel in midair. Both are removed from the siege.
- The party does not control who Tarkanan kills. If Deneith and Aurum are both present, Oleander chooses the one he hates more. If only one is present, that one takes the hit.
- **Complication:** Tarkanan's attack is indiscriminate. Bystanders may die. The party's shifter neighbors, Riddick's urchins — anyone near the target is in danger. This is the cost of using Tarkanan.

### 3. The Tyrants vs. Everyone — "The Double Game"
**The tension:** The Tyrants are playing every side. They have operatives embedded in Boromar, Daask, and the Aurum's staff. They're feeding intelligence to the party (Leara's deal) while simultaneously selling information to the other factions about the party's defenses. The Tyrants don't want anyone to win — they want everyone weakened.

**How the party exploits it:**
- Expose a Tyrant operative in another faction's camp. The Tyrants don't care about the operative — but the exposed faction will turn on the Tyrants.
- Feed Leara false information about the party's weakness (e.g., "the Bazaar door is unguarded") and watch the Tyrants sell it to a rival faction, leading them into an ambush.
- Make a deal with Leara: "We'll give you exclusive access to copy the schema if you pull your operatives out of the battle entirely." She'll take the deal — and honor it, because the Tyrants value information more than violence.

**What happens when it breaks:**
- If a Tyrant operative is exposed, the host faction attacks them. This creates chaos in that faction's ranks (1d4 members turn on each other for 2 rounds).
- If Leara withdraws Tyrant support, the party loses their intelligence stream but the other factions lose their coordination. All enemy waves gain a 1-round delay (factions can't agree on who attacks when).
- The Tyrants never fully commit to fighting. Even if exposed, they pull back and watch. They are the last faction standing after everyone else bleeds — unless the party made a deal that explicitly includes them.

### 4. The Aurum vs. The Syndicates — "Old Money, New Blood"
**The tension:** Lord Vex considers himself above the syndicates. He's here to acquire the schema through wealth and influence, not street violence. The syndicates despise him — a rich man in a skycoach watching them die for a prize he intends to buy.

**How the party exploits it:**
- Shout a negotiation offer to Vex that the syndicates can hear: "Half a million gold if you pull your forces out!" Vex will actually consider it — and the syndicates will realize he's not on their side.
- Point out that Vex is waiting for the syndicates to weaken each other so the Aurum can sweep in. This is true. Corrin and Garra are smart enough to realize it.
- Attack Vex's skycoach. If the party damages it, Vex withdraws — he didn't sign up to die. But if the syndicates see the party attacking Vex, they might interpret it as an alliance offer.

**What happens when it breaks:**
- Vex's skycoach withdraws to a safer distance. The Aurum becomes an observer, not a participant.
- The syndicates briefly unite in contempt for Vex — this is bad for the party if they wanted chaos, good if they wanted a moment of breathing room while everyone yells at the skycoach.
- If the party negotiates with Vex successfully (DC 18 Charisma (Persuasion) + a credible offer), he pulls his forces entirely and offers to buy the schema "when this unpleasantness is over." This removes the Aurum from the siege and creates a potential post-siege deal.

### 5. House Deneith vs. Its Own Orders — "The Marshal's Conscience"
**The tension:** The Deneith marshals on site are under orders to "contain the situation" — which means prevent the schema from leaving Sharn. They are not authorized to attack civilians. They are not authorized to work with syndicates. Their commanding marshal is getting increasingly uncomfortable with the coalition.

**How the party exploits it:**
- If Serik is present: Serik can confront his fellow Deneith marshal. *"You're standing shoulder to shoulder with Daask. Look at yourself."* DC 15 Charisma (Persuasion) by Serik (or the party backing him up) to make the Deneith forces withdraw.
- Point out that civilians are in danger — the shifter community, the urchins, the baker. Deneith Sentinels are sworn to protect civilians. If the party makes this argument (DC 15 Charisma (Persuasion)), the marshals shift to civilian protection rather than siege participation.
- If Tarkanan attacks Deneith: the marshals have justification to engage Tarkanan — which means they're fighting a syndicate instead of the party.

**What happens when it breaks:**
- Deneith withdraws from the siege entirely. They form a perimeter around civilian areas and let the syndicates and the party kill each other.
- The Deneith commander sends a sending stone message to their superiors. Higher-level Deneith forces may arrive in 8+ hours — but that's after the siege is decided.
- If Serik is the one who convinced them: his Deneith career is over. He knows this. He does it anyway.

## The Coalition Escalation Track

The DM tracks coalition stability on a simple scale. Start at **Stable (5)**. Each fault line that breaks reduces stability by 1-2 points. At certain thresholds, the siege changes:

| Stability | Coalition State | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **5 — Stable** | United front | All factions attack in coordinated waves. Maximum difficulty. |
| **4 — Cracks** | Grumbling, delays | Waves have 1-round gaps between them. Factions argue over who attacks next. |
| **3 — Fracturing** | Open arguments | One faction (DM's choice) attacks another faction instead of the party for 1d4 rounds. |
| **2 — Breaking** | Chaos | Two factions are fighting each other. Remaining factions attack with half strength. |
| **1 — Collapsing** | Every faction for itself | All remaining factions prioritize self-preservation. Waves stop. Individual skirmishes only. |
| **0 — Rout** | Siege broken | Factions retreat. The party wins. The cellar door opens. |

**How stability decreases:**
- Each fault line triggered: -1 to -2 (DM discretion based on how explosively it breaks)
- Major faction leader killed or routed: -1
- Party successfully repels a full wave without losing an ally: -1 (the coalition loses morale)
- Party negotiates a faction's withdrawal: -1

**How stability increases (yes, it can):**
- A party ally is killed or captured: +1 (the coalition is emboldened)
- The shard is seen to react/pulse: +1 (reminds everyone what they're fighting for)
- A faction leader makes a rousing speech: +1 (DM can use this to ratchet tension back up)

## DM Notes
- The fault lines are the siege's main mechanic. Don't hide them — let the party discover them through observation, roleplay, and intelligence from allies.
- Don't force the party to trigger every fault line. 2-3 well-timed fractures should be enough to turn the tide from unwinnable to manageable.
- The coalition stability track is a pacing tool. If the party is struggling, make fault lines easier to trigger. If they're breezing through, increase stability by having a faction leader rally the coalition.
- When stability hits 0, the siege doesn't instantly end — it takes 2-3 rounds for the chaos to fully dissolve. Use those rounds for a final triumphant beat: Torvin shoving the last ogre out the belfry window, Serik disarming the Boromar lieutenant, the party standing together in the wreckage of their taproom.
- The sealed Dhakaani door in the cellar starts glowing when stability hits 1. By 0, it's fully open. The party doesn't open it — *the shard does.*
